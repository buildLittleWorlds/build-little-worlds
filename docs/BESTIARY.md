# A Bestiary of Worlds

### Seven cultivated citation-worlds for the Build Little Worlds lab

*Companion to `MODULE.md` and `COMBINATOR.md`. Read the module first if you want
the theory; this is the worked stock — finished worlds you can take apart,
assign, fork, or collide further.*

---

## What this is, and how to use it

A bestiary, traditionally, is a book of creatures: each entry a named animal,
its nature, its habits, and the lesson it carries. This is a bestiary of
*worlds*. Each entry is a small scholarly civilization grown from the citation
primitive — built not by drawing a map but by setting a rule about how knowledge
is acknowledged and letting the consequences come back.

Every world here is built the way the lab builds: as a **stack of protocols**
that are then **collided** so their contradictions surface, with the
**emergent customs** scholars would invent to survive those contradictions, a
**sample text** written under the combined rules so you can hear the world
speak, and the **dissent** the world raises against itself. Nothing in an entry
is decorative. Each piece is a part you could pull out and recombine.

These are not the "right answers." They are seeds. The point of a bestiary is
not to admire the animals but to learn to recognize and breed new ones. Read an
entry, then ask the lab's four questions of it — *what counts as a source here?
who is obligated, and who may refuse? what happens when citation fails? what
scholarship does this make possible?* — and you will find the seams where you
could open it up and grow something else.

A note for the reader who is an AI or a developer: each entry is also a worked
demonstration of the design principles in `MODULE.md` Part III. Notice that
every world is legible as parts, that every collision *diverges* rather than
resolves, and that every emergent custom carries an implicit seed that could be
promoted into the next round. The bestiary is what the system is *for*; it is
also a test of whether the system is behaving.

### How to read an entry

Each dossier has a fixed shape:

- **Premise** — the world in a sentence.
- **The stack** — the protocols it is built from, each with its parts.
- **The collision** — where the protocols cannot both be obeyed, and the exact
  situation that forces a choice.
- **Emergent customs** — what scholars invent to live inside the contradiction.
- **A text from this world** — a short artifact written under the combined
  rules, so the world is heard and not just described.
- **The world's dissent** — the strongest objection the world raises against
  itself, because a custom that survives one contradiction always creates
  another.
- **What it exercises** — which of the eight dimensions this world is mainly a
  study of, for the reader building a syllabus or a coverage map.

## The seven

1. **The Citadel of Unpreserved Sources** — where you must confess what you lost,
   and may not name what refused you.
2. **The Solvent Academy** — where citing is borrowing, and knowledge forgotten
   is debt forgiven.
3. **The Summoning Halls** — where to cite the dead is to call them, and some of
   the dead may not be called.
4. **The Witness Republic** — where nothing is true until someone living will
   stand for it.
5. **The Skin Archive** — where your debts are worn, and one source may never be
   marked.
6. **The Inheritance Courts** — where you cite your ancestors and answer for
   their errors.
7. **The Performed Canon** — where a claim is real only while it is still being
   enacted.

## I. The Citadel of Unpreserved Sources

> *Where you must confess what you lost, and may not name what refused you.*

**Premise.** A university where every citation must declare what the scholar
*failed to carry forward* from a source — and where any source that has refused
citation may never be named at all. Honesty about loss and obedience to silence
are both absolute, and they do not fit.

**The stack.**

- *The Unpreserved Source Rule* (protocol). Every citation names the part of the
  source the scholar could not preserve. **Parts:** the *loss clause* (the
  declared omission), the *witness mark* (a sign that the loss was examined by
  another), the *repair interval* (a period during which the loss may be made
  good by a later scholar).
- *The Law of Silent Sources* (refusal custom). A source that has refused
  citation may never be named, quoted, or pointed to. **Parts:** the *refusal
  registry* (the list of the withdrawn), the *naming taboo* (the prohibition
  itself), the *silent footnote* (an acknowledged blank where a citation would
  be).

**The collision.** The loss clause requires you to name the source you failed to
preserve. The silence rule forbids naming a refused source at all. *The stakes:*
a scholar who has drawn on a refused source cannot complete the loss clause
without breaking the silence — and cannot keep silent without falsifying the
record of loss. Both rules are reasonable. Together they are a trap, and every
serious scholar eventually steps in it.

**Emergent customs.**

- *The Sealed Loss.* Scholars record *that* something was lost without naming
  *what* — a sealed mark, honest about the omission, silent about its object.
  Born from the loss clause reconciled with the naming taboo. (Seed: *a world
  where omissions are recorded but their objects stay unnamed.*)
- *The Confessors.* A small office of trusted readers permitted to know what a
  sealed loss conceals, so that the repair interval is not wholly dead. They
  carry the named losses to their graves. To be made a Confessor is the
  Citadel's highest and least wanted honor.

**A text from this world.**

> From the closing apparatus of a dissertation, *On the Migration of Grief:*
>
> §41. Here I relied on a source I am forbidden to name. ⟁ *Sealed.* I can say
> only that I failed to preserve its argument about the second mourning, and
> that the failure was examined. The Confessor of this faculty holds what the
> seal holds. If, in some freer century, the source withdraws its refusal, let
> the repair fall to whoever reads this then; I could not make it.

**The world's dissent.** A sealed loss that hides its object cannot be repaired
by anyone but its author and the Confessor who outlives them — so the repair
interval, the rule's one hope, quietly dies, and in this world *loss becomes
permanent by design.* The Citadel's great virtue, its honesty about what it
drops, is precisely what guarantees the drop can never be recovered.

**What it exercises.** Protocol; refusal custom; memory (as the politics of the
unrecoverable).

## II. The Solvent Academy

> *Where citing is borrowing, and knowledge forgotten is debt forgiven.*

**Premise.** A scholarly culture in which to cite someone is not to honor them
but to *owe* them — a debt of labor and defense entered in a public ledger — and
in which any claim that goes uncited long enough *decays out of the record
entirely*, taking its debts with it. Scholars must borrow to think and go broke
by remembering.

**The stack.**

- *The Citation Ledger* (obligation). Every citation is a debt: the citer owes
  the cited future labor, public defense, and a share of any credit the work
  earns. **Parts:** the *debt entry*, the *defense clause* (you must defend what
  you cite if it is attacked), the *solvency review* (periodic audit of whether
  a scholar can cover their debts).
- *The Fading Record* (memory system). A claim not re-cited within a set period
  decays and is struck from the citeable record; once struck, it cannot be
  borrowed from again. **Parts:** the *decay clock*, the *strike*, the *salvage
  ban* (struck claims may not be revived).

**The collision.** To stay intellectually solvent, a scholar should cite
little — every citation is a liability. But a claim survives only by being
re-cited; stop borrowing from it and the decay clock runs out and it is struck.
*The stakes:* the claims most worth preserving are precisely the ones that
demand the most citation to survive, which means preserving important knowledge
is the fastest way to bankrupt yourself. Care and solvency point in opposite
directions.

**Emergent customs.**

- *The Keeping Funds.* Scholars pool their citational debt into shared
  endowments that cite endangered claims collectively, so no single person bears
  the liability of keeping an important idea alive. Born from the defense clause
  meeting the decay clock. (Seed: *a world where the duty to remember is
  insured collectively.*)
- *Mercy Strikes.* It becomes a recognized kindness to *let* a rival's flawed
  claim decay rather than re-citing it to refute it — since even a hostile
  citation is a debt and a life-support. To attack an idea publicly is to keep
  it alive; the cruelest move is silence.

**A text from this world.**

> Minutes of the Third Keeping Fund, on the claim *that memory has a grain:*
>
> Moved: that the Fund assume the debt of citation for the grain-of-memory
> claim, whose decay clock expires at winter. Noted: the claim has three living
> dependents who cannot afford it alone. Noted further: Scholar Vey requests we
> let her own early error on the same subject go to strike, unrefuted. The Fund
> assumes the first debt and grants the mercy. Let the grain be kept and the
> error forgotten, which in this house are the same vote taken twice.

**The world's dissent.** If remembering is a debt and forgetting is forgiveness,
then the Academy's economy rewards *strategic forgetting* — and a culture that
can be made solvent by letting knowledge die will, under pressure, let exactly
the knowledge it most needs die first, because that knowledge costs the most to
keep. The Keeping Funds slow this but cannot reverse its logic: solvency is
still purchased in forgetting.

**What it exercises.** Obligation; memory system; institution (the Fund as the
world's immune response).

## III. The Summoning Halls

> *Where to cite the dead is to call them, and some of the dead may not be
> called.*

**Premise.** A discipline in which citation is *summoning*: to cite a scholar,
living or dead, is to bind their position to appear and answer in the present
argument. But certain sources have been granted *sanctuary* — the right never to
be summoned — and the line between a citation and a violation is thin and
contested.

**The stack.**

- *The Summons* (authority rule). To cite is to call: the cited position must be
  represented as if present and made to answer the citer's challenge. **Parts:**
  the *calling* (the formal citation-summons), the *answer* (the obligation to
  state the cited's strongest reply before disagreeing), the *binding cost*
  (summoning is effortful and cannot be done lightly).
- *The Right of Sanctuary* (refusal custom). A source may be placed beyond
  summons — by its author, or by a guardian after death — and may then be neither
  cited nor answered. **Parts:** the *sanctuary grant*, the *guardian* (who holds
  the right for the dead), the *trespass* (an unlawful summons of a sanctuaried
  source).

**The collision.** The Summons requires that before disagreeing with a position
you state its strongest answer — you must let the cited speak. But a sanctuaried
source may not be summoned, so its position can be neither invoked nor answered.
*The stakes:* a scholar who must engage an idea that originates in a sanctuaried
source cannot state its defense without trespass, and cannot honestly argue
against it while pretending it does not exist. The most important objections are
often the ones you are forbidden to call.

**Emergent customs.**

- *Speaking Around.* Scholars develop an art of arguing with a sanctuaried
  position *without naming or summoning it* — addressing the shape of an idea
  through its visible effects on other, callable scholars. A whole rhetoric of
  the deliberately unnamed. Born from the answer obligation meeting the
  sanctuary grant. (Seed: *a world where the most important ideas can only be
  argued with indirectly.*)
- *The Guardians' Bargain.* Guardians of the sanctuaried dead may, for a price
  of obligation, grant a single supervised summons — a controlled séance in which
  the protected position is called once, answers once, and is sealed again.
  Access to the most important minds becomes a guarded, rationed thing.

**A text from this world.**

> Opening of a disputation, the challenger speaking:
>
> I call Aurel of the western school, and I state her answer before I strike it:
> she would say that a proof witnessed by one is a proof. *She is called; she has
> answered.* — As for the deeper objection, the one that truly threatens my
> argument, I may not call its author; she rests in sanctuary. So I will speak
> around her. You will know the shape of what I cannot summon by the way the rest
> of the field still leans toward where she stood.

**The world's dissent.** Sanctuary was meant to protect the dead from being
endlessly conscripted into living quarrels — a mercy. But by making the strongest
ideas the ones that cannot be summoned, it ensures that the most important
thinking in the discipline happens *around an absence*, in indirection and
rumor, never in the open. The world protects its greatest minds by guaranteeing
they can never be fully heard.

**What it exercises.** Authority rule; refusal custom; evidence ritual (the
summons-and-answer as the rite that makes a claim contestable).

## IV. The Witness Republic

> *Where nothing is true until someone living will stand for it.*

**Premise.** A republic of scholars in which no claim is citeable until a *living
witness* stands and reaffirms it before the work is read — and in which the right
to stand for a dead scholar's claims is *inherited*, passed down like an estate.
Authority lives in bodies, not in texts, and dies with them unless an heir takes
it up.

**The stack.**

- *The Standing Oath* (authority rule). A claim becomes citeable only when a
  living witness rises and reaffirms it; an unreaffirmed claim has no standing.
  **Parts:** the *standing* (the act of rising to affirm), the *reaffirmation
  quorum* (how many must stand for a contested claim), the *lapse* (a claim whose
  witnesses have all died and goes silent).
- *The Inherited Voice* (institution). The right to stand for a dead scholar's
  claims passes to a named heir, who may reaffirm in the dead one's place.
  **Parts:** the *succession* (the naming of an heir), the *estate of claims*
  (the body of positions one may stand for), the *disinheritance* (the stripping
  of an heir who reaffirms falsely).

**The collision.** The Standing Oath makes truth depend on a living body willing
to rise. The Inherited Voice lets a body rise for claims it did not make and may
not understand. *The stakes:* an heir may be obligated to stand for an ancestor's
claim they privately believe is wrong — and to refuse is to let the whole estate
of claims lapse into silence, betraying a lineage; while to stand is to lend
their living authority to a falsehood. Loyalty to the dead and honesty in the
present cannot both be served at the witness bench.

**Emergent customs.**

- *Standing Under Protest.* An heir may rise for an ancestral claim while
  formally marking that they stand *for the lineage, not the truth of it* — a
  recognized half-affirmation that keeps the claim citeable but flags it as
  unloved by its own witness. Born from the standing oath meeting the inherited
  voice. (Seed: *a world where you can vouch for who said a thing without
  vouching that it is true.*)
- *The Lapsing Festivals.* Once a generation, the republic deliberately lets
  certain claims lapse — declines to stand for them — in public ceremony, as a
  way of pruning the inheritance. To choose what to let die is the republic's
  most solemn collective act, and the most political.

**A text from this world.**

> Recorded at the witness bench, the heir of the house of Sorel rising:
>
> I stand for the claim of my grandmother, that the tides answer to grief. *I
> stand under protest.* Let the record show the claim keeps its standing by my
> body and not by my belief; I rise for the house of Sorel, who said it, and not
> for the tides, who I think do not listen. If a better witness will stand
> plainly, I will sit gladly and let my protest fall.

**The world's dissent.** If truth requires a living body to stand for it, then
the republic's knowledge is only ever as large as its population is willing and
able to vouch — and *Standing Under Protest*, invented to let honest heirs keep
lineages alive, slowly fills the record with claims everyone keeps citeable and
no one actually believes. The world's archive becomes a crowd of positions held
up by reluctant hands, true in standing and hollow in fact.

**What it exercises.** Authority rule; institution; evidence ritual (standing as
the rite that confers citeability).

## V. The Skin Archive

> *Where your debts are worn, and one source may never be marked.*

**Premise.** A scholarly order in which citations are not written but *worn* —
inscribed on the body as marks, so that a person's intellectual debts can be read
off their skin — and in which one source, the *founding source*, may never be
marked by anyone, on pain of erasure. The bibliography is a body, and it has a
blasphemy.

**The stack.**

- *The Worn Citation* (source interface). Every debt of citation is inscribed on
  the citer's body; to know what a scholar has drawn on, you read them. **Parts:**
  the *marking* (the act of inscription), the *reading* (interpreting a scholar's
  marks), the *forgery* (a false or stolen mark, detectable by inspection).
- *The Unmarked Founder* (refusal custom). The founding source of the order may
  never be inscribed; all owe it, none may wear it. **Parts:** the *unmarked
  debt* (the universal, invisible obligation), the *blank* (the place on the body
  where the founder's mark would go, kept bare), the *erasure* (the penalty for
  marking the founder).

**The collision.** The Worn Citation makes intellectual honesty *physically
legible* — your debts are on you, and to hide a debt is to forge. The Unmarked
Founder makes the largest, most universal debt of all *unwearable* — everyone
owes the founder, and no one may show it. *The stakes:* every scholar walks
around bearing a debt they are forbidden to display, which means every honest
body is, by the rule's own standard, a forgery — incomplete, hiding its deepest
source. The world's central virtue, legibility, indicts everyone.

**Emergent customs.**

- *The Kept Blank.* Scholars learn to wear the *absence* of the founder's mark as
  a mark — keeping one place on the body deliberately, conspicuously bare, so that
  the blank itself reads as "here is the debt I may not show." Born from the
  marking obligation meeting the blasphemy. (Seed: *a world where a deliberate
  blank is itself a kind of writing.*)
- *Skin-Readers.* A profession arises of those trained to read bodies fluently —
  to authenticate marks, detect forgeries, and, most delicately, to confirm that
  a scholar's Kept Blank is honestly bare and not concealing a trespass. To be
  read by a Skin-Reader is the world's form of peer review.

**A text from this world.**

> A Skin-Reader's attestation, inscribed (where else) on the Reader's own arm:
>
> I have read the scholar Tomas, forearm to shoulder, and find his debts true:
> nine marks, each answering to a work I can name, none forged. At the hollow of
> the throat he keeps the Blank, bare and well-kept, as we all must for the one
> we may not wear. His honesty is complete in the only way honesty is permitted
> to be complete here — which is to say, with a hole in it, in the agreed place.

**The world's dissent.** A system that makes debt legible on the body promises
that nothing can be hidden — yet it is founded on a debt that *must* be hidden,
and the Kept Blank turns that founding concealment into a celebrated practice. So
the Skin Archive teaches its scholars, in their most honest act, to wear an
absence proudly; it makes a virtue of the one omission it cannot inspect, and
thereby trains everyone in the elegant hiding of exactly one thing. What else, a
heretic asks, might be hidden in a well-kept blank?

**What it exercises.** Source interface; refusal custom; authority (legibility of
the body as the seat of trust).

## VI. The Inheritance Courts

> *Where you cite your ancestors and answer for their errors.*

**Premise.** A jurisdiction of scholars in which citation is *kinship*: to cite a
work is to claim descent from it and take on the duties of an heir — and in which
*liability flows down the line of descent*, so that if a cited claim is later
found false, the error becomes the citer's to answer for. You inherit your
sources, and you inherit their debts.

**The stack.**

- *The Line of Descent* (obligation). To cite is to descend: the citer becomes
  heir to the cited, owing it the duties of kinship — tending, defense,
  continuation. **Parts:** the *claim of descent* (the citation as adoption into
  a lineage), the *duty of tending* (the upkeep of the ancestor's reputation),
  the *continuation* (the obligation to carry the work forward).
- *The Flow of Liability* (authority rule). When a claim is found false, the
  fault descends the citation line: heirs answer for the errors of those they
  cited. **Parts:** the *finding* (the judgment that a claim is false), the
  *descent of fault* (the assignment of liability to the heirs), the
  *disavowal* (a costly, formal severing of descent to escape inherited fault).

**The collision.** The Line of Descent rewards wide, loyal citation — the more
ancestors you claim, the richer your lineage and standing. The Flow of Liability
punishes exactly that — every ancestor you claim is an error you might inherit.
*The stakes:* a scholar must choose between a proud, well-descended argument
exposed to the faults of all its ancestors, and a thin, defensible argument that
disavows its way to safety and stands almost alone. Honoring your influences and
protecting yourself from them are the same decision made in opposite directions.

**Emergent customs.**

- *The Quiet Lines.* Scholars maintain unofficial, undeclared descents — drawing
  on ancestors they do not formally cite, to gain the inheritance of ideas
  without the inheritance of liability. A shadow genealogy runs beneath every
  visible one. Born from the claim of descent meeting the descent of fault.
  (Seed: *a world where the influences you hide are the ones you most rely on.*)
- *Deathbed Disavowals.* Aging scholars formally disavow their most exposed
  ancestors near the end, severing risky lines so their own heirs inherit a
  cleaner estate. To die well, in the Courts, is to die defensible — and the
  disavowal of one's own teachers becomes a recognized, sorrowful rite of late
  career.

**A text from this world.**

> Filed before the Inheritance Court, the petition of the scholar Ren:
>
> I claim descent from Halvic, and from Halvic's master, and from three of the
> western proofs, and I take up the tending of all four. — But I disavow, this
> day, my descent from the river-school, whose central finding the Court has
> ruled false. Let the fault not flow to me or to those who will one day cite me.
> I sever the line. I know what it costs; I knew the river-school's work before I
> knew my own name, and I will not name it again.

**The world's dissent.** A regime that makes you answer for your ancestors'
errors promises rigor — no one may cite carelessly. But by pricing every honest
acknowledgment as a risk, it drives the real intellectual descent underground
into the Quiet Lines, so that the *declared* genealogy of the discipline becomes
a careful fiction and the *true* one is never written. The Courts make scholars
responsible for their influences by teaching them to hide their influences; the
record of who descends from whom becomes the one thing the world systematically
falsifies.

**What it exercises.** Obligation; authority rule; institution (the Court as the
adjudicator of descent and fault).

## VII. The Performed Canon

> *Where a claim is real only while it is still being enacted.*

**Premise.** A tradition in which a claim is not citeable as text but only as
*performance* — it must be enacted (sung, walked, built, cooked, danced) to count
as real — and in which a claim that goes unperformed long enough *fades from the
canon*. Knowledge here is not stored; it is *done*, and what is not done is
forgotten.

**The stack.**

- *The Enactment* (evidence ritual). A claim becomes citeable only by being
  performed; to cite is to perform, or to attend a performance and attest it.
  **Parts:** the *performance* (the enacting of the claim), the *attestation*
  (the witness's record that it was enacted), the *form* (the specific medium —
  song, walk, dish — proper to a given claim).
- *The Fading Canon* (memory system). A claim unperformed for a set period fades
  and leaves the canon; faded claims may be re-performed only if someone still
  remembers the form. **Parts:** the *fade clock*, the *forgetting* (the loss of
  the form, not just the claim), the *recovery* (the rare reconstruction of a
  lost performance).

**The collision.** The Enactment ties a claim's reality to a specific form — this
song, this walk, this dish *is* the claim. The Fading Canon lets forms be lost
when performances lapse. *The stakes:* when the last person who knows the form of
an important claim grows old, the claim's survival depends on transmitting a
performance that cannot be written down without ceasing to be the claim — and a
generation that fails to learn the dance does not merely forget a fact; it
*unmakes* a truth. Preservation and embodiment pull against each other: to save
a claim you would write it, but written it is no longer real.

**Emergent customs.**

- *The Carriers.* Certain people devote their lives to holding the forms of
  endangered claims in their bodies — learning the rare dances and dishes whose
  performers are dying — so the canon survives in living memory rather than dead
  record. To be a Carrier is to *be* a citation, walking. Born from the form
  meeting the fade clock. (Seed: *a world where some people are libraries made of
  muscle.*)
- *Deliberate Variations.* Because a form transmitted perfectly is fragile —
  one broken chain and it is gone — performers begin teaching *several* valid
  variants of an important claim's form, so the claim can survive the loss of any
  single version. Redundancy through controlled imperfection: the truth is kept
  alive by being allowed to differ.

**A text from this world.**

> Spoken by an old Carrier to a young one, which is itself the attestation:
>
> Watch my feet, not my words; the words are not the claim. This walk is the
> proof that the marsh can be crossed in flood, and it has been walked, witness
> to witness, since before the towers. I carry three ways to walk it now — the
> short, the safe, and the old hard way — because if you learn only one and break
> your leg in a hard winter, the marsh becomes uncrossable again, and a true
> thing leaves the world through your one ankle. Learn all three. Then find
> someone to watch *your* feet.

**The world's dissent.** A canon that lives only in performance is gloriously
alive — nothing in it is dead text, everything is enacted and witnessed. But it
is also one bad winter, one broken chain of Carriers, from amnesia, and the
Deliberate Variations meant to protect it ensure that no two performances of a
claim are identical — so over generations the claim *drifts*, and the marsh-walk
the great-grandchildren attest is no longer quite the one the ancestors proved.
The world keeps its truths alive only by letting them slowly become other truths.
Nothing is forgotten outright; everything is forgotten by degrees.

**What it exercises.** Evidence ritual; memory system; source interface (the
performing body as the medium of citation).

## Coverage, and how to keep breeding

The seven worlds are not a random menagerie. Between them they put weight on each
of the eight dimensions named in `MODULE.md`, so that a reader working through
the bestiary meets every lever of the citation primitive at least once, pulled
hard:

- **Protocol** — Citadel (the loss clause as the spine of a world).
- **Obligation** — Solvent Academy, Inheritance Courts (debt; inherited
  liability).
- **Evidence ritual** — Summoning Halls, Witness Republic, Performed Canon
  (summons, standing, enactment).
- **Memory system** — Solvent Academy, Performed Canon, and the Citadel's
  unrecoverable losses (decay, fading, the unrepairable).
- **Authority rule** — Summoning Halls, Witness Republic, Inheritance Courts,
  Skin Archive (who confers citeability: the called, the standing, the descended,
  the legible body).
- **Refusal custom** — Citadel, Summoning Halls, Skin Archive (silence,
  sanctuary, the unmarked founder).
- **Source interface** — Skin Archive, Performed Canon (the body as
  bibliography; the body as performance).
- **Institution** — Solvent Academy (the Keeping Funds), Witness Republic (the
  Inherited Voice), Inheritance Courts (the Court).

Three patterns are worth naming for anyone who wants to build the eighth world
and beyond.

First, **every world's dissent is a seed.** Each entry ends by showing how the
custom that resolved one contradiction quietly produced another — the Citadel's
honesty guaranteeing permanent loss, the Courts' rigor driving descent
underground. That second contradiction is exactly where you would collide again.
A dissent is not a closing; it is the next round's opening move.

Second, **the worlds can be collided with each other, not only built alone.**
Push the Skin Archive against the Witness Republic and ask whether a mark on a
body can stand for a claim, or whether only a living voice can — the worn
citation against the standing oath. Push the Performed Canon against the Solvent
Academy and ask what debt is owed for *attending* a performance that keeps a
claim alive. The bestiary is itself a bench. Every entry is a stack you can lift
whole and drop into a new collision.

Third, **the strongest new worlds usually come from an empty dimension.** Notice
which lever a world barely touches and set *that* one to an extreme. None of the
seven, for instance, makes its primary study the moment a citation *lies* — a
world built on the deliberate false citation, the planted source, the forged
descent, would put weight where these seven are light. The gaps in the coverage
map are the most fertile ground in the book.

A bestiary is finished only in the sense that the page count ran out. These
seven are meant to be taken apart. Read one until you can hear its scholars
arguing, then find the seam — the dissent, the empty dimension, the collision
with a neighbor — and grow the world the book left out.

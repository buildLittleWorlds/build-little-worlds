# Little Worlds and Large Language Models

Build Little Worlds is not only a name for a website. It is a way of thinking.

Finite minds often learn large orders by making small, bounded worlds. A syllable, a token, a word, an equation, a prime number, a diagram, a parable, a proof, a laboratory setup: each one limits the full complexity of reality so that some pattern can become visible.

The little world is not a retreat from the large world. It is a way into it.

## The old movement: small things disclose large orders

Theology, mathematics, and science all depend on this movement from small to large.

A seed can teach us about growth. A parable can open a kingdom. A triangle can reveal relations that hold far beyond the drawn figure. A prime number can become a doorway into the structure of number itself. A controlled experiment can isolate one relation inside a world too complex to understand all at once.

The small thing matters because it is bounded. We can look at it. We can repeat it. We can vary it. We can ask what it preserves and what it leaves out.

This is microcosm thinking: the little world helps disclose the larger one.

## The LLM reversal: small operations scale into large powers

Large language models add a second movement.

They begin with little things: tokens, vectors, probabilities, gradients, losses. A token is not a sentence. A probability distribution is not wisdom. A vector is not meaning. A gradient is not understanding.

But the small operations do not stay small.

When they are repeated at vast scale across enormous bodies of text, they begin to disclose larger powers of language: analogy, tone, genre, translation, style, instruction-following, compression, imitation, and patterns of reasoning.

The little world becomes interesting not only because it abstracts from the large world, but because scaling many little operations can reveal new large-world behavior.

## The theological question

Christian thought has long moved between little and large: seed and kingdom, word and Word, sign and reality, creature and creation, microcosm and macrocosm.

LLMs do not replace that tradition. They give it a new technical object.

They ask us to think about how bounded forms disclose larger orders, how tiny linguistic units bear relation to whole worlds of meaning, and how scale can transform the power of small mathematical operations.

That is why Build Little Worlds is a fitting home for Theology of LLMs. The site is not simply a little world about a topic. It is an inquiry into the strange, old, and newly urgent fact that little worlds can teach us how large worlds are ordered.

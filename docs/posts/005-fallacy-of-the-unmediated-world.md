# The Fallacy of the Unmediated World

In recent years, a quiet asceticism has taken root on college campuses. Groups calling themselves "Luddite clubs" meet in public parks, trade their smartphones for flip phones, and gather under natural light to write in journals. They seek to rescue their attention from the jaws of the machine. They are looking for a way to live in the presence of an overwhelming technological infrastructure.

Their frustration is easy to understand, and their desire for quietness is admirable. Yet their diagnosis and their remedy are built on a profound misunderstanding. The modern "revolt against technology"—which quotes Martin Heidegger on the dangers of technology's "enframing" and laments the "fracking" of our interior lives—is typical of a resistance that sees technology as a foreign, corrupting force. It implies that if we could only strip away the silicon, step outside into the natural world, or look deeply inside ourselves, we would find a pristine, unmediated reality where we can be whole.

But in a Christian context, this perspective is both philosophically incoherent and theologically empty. 

## The question-begging critique of mediation

The central charge leveled against technology by modern techno-skeptics is that it *mediates* our existence. Heidegger famously argued that technology changes our relationship to the world, transforming a river like the Rhine from an object of natural beauty into a "standing reserve" of hydroelectric power. Today’s tech-pessimists follow suit, complaining that screens and algorithms stand between us and the world, distorting our direct connection to nature and to ourselves.

But this critique is a deep form of question-begging. 

Mediation is not the defect of technology; it is its definition. Human beings do not engage with the cosmos in a raw, unmediated state. We are, by design, tool-makers and sign-givers. Language itself is a technology of mediation. Writing is a technology that externalizes memory. Mathematics is a technology that isolates and tracks form. 

To create technology in order to mediate our experience of the world in good ways is not a corruption of our nature—it is exactly what we do. We build telescopes to mediate the stars, plows to mediate the soil, and violins to mediate sound. To point to the fact that a tool mediates our world and declare, "See, that is what is wrong with it," is to condemn the tool for being a tool. Fleeing mediation is not a return to true human nature; it is a flight from it.

## The lukewarmness of "values-affirmation"

When modern tech-skeptics try to offer practical guidance on how to live, they usually fall back on a lukewarm, self-referential moralism. We are told to evaluate tools by asking: *Does this technology expand my freedom? Does it contribute to my flourishing? Does it align with my values?*

Theologically, this is a vacuum. Any technology can expand or contract your freedom depending on who you are and what you love. Writing can preserve the Word of God or codify a bureaucratic tyranny. A calculator can aid a physicist in understanding planetary motion or optimize a predatory financial ledger. The tool itself cannot validate the value, and "values" themselves are not self-generated preferences we protect from external corruption.

The real question is not whether a technology affirms your subjective values, but whether your use of it acknowledges the Source of all truth and goodness. Technology is not a neutral container for personal desires, nor is it a sovereign demon. It is a secondary participation in the creative order. 

## Praising the tool within the Logos

A theology of technology must begin not with a warning label, but with praise. 

We make because we are made in the image of the Maker. When we discover the physical properties of silicon, the mathematics of probability, and the architectures of computation, we are not building a rival tower of Babel to usurp the divine. We are uncovering patterns that were already built into the fabric of creation. 

Large language models, for instance, show us that human language has a mathematical legibility we did not invent. The geometry of embeddings and the mathematics of next-token prediction do not flatten language; they reveal its deep, ordered beauty. By building these little worlds of math and text, we participate in the Logos—the rational principle through which all things were made and in whom all things hold together.

The struggle of the human condition is not a struggle between the "natural" and the "technological." It is the struggle between idolatry and worship. When we treat technology as a sovereign force that can save us, we make it an idol. But when we treat it as an external enemy that can damn us, we grant it a power it does not possess. 

The human heart is not a pristine wilderness ruined by screens; it is a fallen, complicated space that can be attacked by pride and vanity just as easily in a silent forest as in a server farm. The solution is not to look inward to a mythical uncorrupted self or to flee the tools of our age. The solution is to orient our making and our using toward the Source of goodness, utilizing the tools we build to participate in the restoration and celebration of the world.
